Nicola Tesla

The man who gives the light to billions people

The inventor of the direct current system, which made it possible to transmit electricity over long distances with low losses.

The Genius Inventor

  • 1856: Born on July 10 in Smiljan, Austrian Empire (modern-day Croatia).
  • 1861: Began his schooling at the primary school in Smiljan.
  • 1862: The Tesla family moved to GospiÄ‡, where Tesla's father worked as a pastor.
  • 1870: Attended the Higher Real Gymnasium in Karlovac.
  • 1875: Enrolled at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz, studying electrical engineering.
  • 1878: Left Graz and broke off relations with his family.
  • 1881: Worked for the Central Telegraph Office in Budapest, Hungary.
  • 1882: Moved to Paris to work for the Continental Edison Company.
  • 1884: Emigrated to the United States and began working for Thomas Edison in New York City.
  • 1885: Developed the concept of alternating current (AC) and left Edison's company after disputes.
  • 1887: Founded the Tesla Electric Company and began working on AC motors and transformers.
  • 1888: Patented the AC induction motor and polyphase system, licensing them to George Westinghouse.
  • 1891: Invented the Tesla coil, a high-voltage, high-frequency transformer.
  • 1893: Demonstrated wireless energy transmission at the World Columbian Exposition in Chicago.
  • 1895: Built the first hydroelectric power plant at Niagara Falls in collaboration with Westinghouse.
  • 1899: Conducted high-voltage, high-frequency experiments in Colorado Springs, discovering terrestrial stationary waves.
  • 1900: Began construction of the Wardenclyffe Tower, intended for transatlantic wireless communication and power transmission.
  • 1901: Faced financial difficulties leading to the abandonment of the Wardenclyffe project.
  • 1917: Wardenclyffe Tower was demolished.
  • 1920s-1930s: Tesla's financial situation deteriorated, though he continued to work on various projects.
  • 1937: Delivered a lecture on his theory of dynamic gravity.
  • 1943: Died on January 7 in New York City, largely forgotten and in relative obscurity.
